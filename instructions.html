Instructions

  • The purpose of this labelling process is to create a 'gold-standard' dataset that very accurately categorises transcripts of handover interactions. The data will be used to evaluate how well a large language model can perform this same task. As such, accuracy and completeness is vital in this labelling process.
  • Given we are wanting to minimise the risk of errors in labelling, 45 minutes of time has been assigned to perform the 3 tasks associated with each of the handover examples. Please ensure that you have completed all aspects of each task and checked your work for accuracy.
  • When you have completed all 3 tasks for a handover example, select the green tick button at the bottom of the screen to move on to the next example.
  • Please also save your work by selecting the save button at the top left of the screen. Once you save your work you will no longer be able to go back to any previous examples.
  • Note: you can’t include the newline symbols in spans you highlight. The workaround is just to highlight up to the symbol and then again on the new line afterwards.

Labelling uncertainty

Category Definition / When to Use Example From Handover Speech
Hedge / Probability Language The speaker indicates partial confidence or doubt about information. “I think ENT reviewed him.”
“He should be going to theatre soon.”
Vague / Qualitative Expression Information is described using imprecise or subjective language. “He looks fine now.”
“Seems okay.”
Unknown fact / Explicit Lack of Knowledge The speaker openly states missing knowledge or incomplete data. “Not sure if consent’s been signed.”
“I don’t know his allergies.”
Indefinite Timing Timing or schedule for an event is vague or lacks precision. “Later today.”
“After the round.”
Source Uncertainty Information relies on a second-hand or unverifiable source. “ENT said he’s on the list.”
“Night nurse told me.”
Procedural Uncertainty Unclear next step in process or lack of explicitly stated plan. “We’ll see how he goes.”
“You might want to check his IV.”
Responsibility Uncertainty A required task or follow-up is mentioned, but it’s unclear who is responsible for performing it. “Bloods to be checked later.”
“Needs review this afternoon.”
